How to get clients & promote your practice

Tested, ROI-ranked channels for an early-career psychologist building a private caseload alongside university & hospital training. Researched June 2026.

TL;DR The short answer

Most reward, most tested, fastest: warm referrals from the people who already trust you (supervisors, hospital doctors, university counsellors, colleagues) + a fully optimised Google Business Profile + 2–3 directory listings. These bring high-intent people who are already looking for a therapist.

Instagram and blogs are worth doing — but they're the long game. They build brand and SEO that compound over 6–18 months. They rarely deliver your first clients. Start them now, but don't depend on them early.

Direct answers to your questions: Yes, blog (for SEO + authority, slow payoff). Yes, do SEO — but local SEO/Google Business first, it's the highest-ROI search play. Instagram yes, as a trust-builder, not a client-search engine.

00 Your unfair advantage

You're embedded in a hospital and a university. That is the single biggest head-start most new therapists don't have. Primary-care physicians, college counselling centres, hospitals and treatment centres are the top sources of warm, well-matched referrals. Most therapists spend years building those relationships from scratch — you're already inside them.

Make this your #1 channel. Everything digital below is to support it (people you meet will Google you before they book).

01 Where therapy clients actually come from

Three buckets. A healthy practice uses all three, weighted toward the first two early on:

  • Warm referrals — doctors, colleagues, supervisors, past clients, friends-of-friends. Highest trust, highest conversion, best fit.
  • High-intent search — people typing "therapist near me" or browsing a directory. 77% of people seeking mental-health support start on Google. They're ready to book now.
  • Brand & content — Instagram, blogs, talks. Builds awareness and trust over time so you're who they think of later.

03 Channel deep-dive

1

Referral network — your biggest lever

Tell the people who already trust you that you're taking private clients. Focus on a small, well-aligned set: your supervisors, hospital physicians (esp. GPs/psychiatrists), university counselling staff, and peer therapists who are full and pass on overflow.

How: a short, warm message + a one-page profile (or your website). Send a handwritten thank-you when someone refers. Do quarterly check-ins. Always ask new clients "how did you hear about me?" and track it.

2

Google Business Profile + local SEO

Free, and often more important than your website for getting found. 77% of clients start on Google; an optimised profile puts you in the local "map pack." No algorithm to fight.

How: complete every field; primary category Psychotherapist; 5+ photos; keyword-rich 750-char description (first 250 matter most); consistent name/address/phone everywhere (NAP); collect recent reviews (8–10 reviews in the last 90 days beats 50 old ones). Results in 4–8 weeks. Needs a service address — see caveats.

3

Therapist directories — pick by your country

Low effort, high intent. People on directories are actively searching for a therapist.

India: TheMindClan (curated, values-based, free — no listing fee or commission; ~1–2 month vetting), and Amaha / Practo for booking visibility.
US: Psychology Today (~$30/mo; still the default, though referrals have softened and you compete with platform-affiliated listings), TherapyDen, Inclusive Therapists, and insurance panels (Alma, Headway) which auto-list you to members searching for care.

4

A website that converts + a sharp niche

Your site (already built) is the hub every other channel points to. Its job: speak to one ideal client, show how you help, and make booking obvious. "You can't market effectively until you know who you're talking to." A niche (e.g., "anxiety & burnout in young professionals") makes every other channel 2–3× more effective.

5

Blogs + SEO — the compounding asset

Blogging sites get 55% more visitors. Posts answering real client questions ("how do I know if I need therapy?", "therapist in [city] for anxiety") rank over time and feed Google. Slow (6–12 months) but it compounds and you own it. Aim for 1 quality, locally-targeted post per month — quality over volume.

6

Talks & workshops — leverage your affiliation

A free talk at your university, a community group, or a corporate wellness session positions you as the local expert and generates direct referrals. Your student/hospital status gives you natural access to host these.

7

Instagram / Reels — brand, not search

32% of people follow mental-health professionals on social; 71% of 18–29s use Instagram, so it's strong for a younger niche. But it's not where people search for a therapist — it builds a following who turn to you later. Expect 12–18 months of consistent posting. Short, genuine spoken videos outperform polished graphics. Pick a niche and post 2–3×/week.

8

Email newsletter — quiet, high-value nurture

Capture interested visitors and stay in touch until they're ready. Low cost, durable, you own the list (unlike followers). A monthly note with one helpful idea keeps you top-of-mind.

9

LinkedIn — professional-to-professional

Useful for building referral relationships with other clinicians and organisations, less for direct client search. Low effort to maintain a credible profile.

04 Verdict — what gives the most reward

For your situation, the tested, highest-reward combination is:
  1. Systematically activate your hospital + university referral network (fastest, best-fit clients).
  2. Claim & fully optimise your Google Business Profile (owns high-intent local search).
  3. List on 2–3 directories for your region (low effort, ready-to-book traffic).
  4. Sharpen your niche & website conversion so everything else lands better.

Then layer the long game — blogs/SEO + Instagram + a newsletter — starting now but with patient expectations. They're how you stop depending on referrals 1–2 years out.

05 Your first 90 days

Weeks 1–2 · Foundations

  • Define your niche & ideal client; reflect it on the website with one clear booking CTA.
  • Create & fully optimise your Google Business Profile.
  • Set up "how did you hear about me?" tracking on your booking form.

Weeks 2–4 · Activate

  • Tell your network you're accepting private clients (supervisors, hospital doctors, uni counsellors, peers) with a short message + profile link.
  • Apply to 2–3 directories for your region.
  • Start collecting Google reviews where ethically permitted.

Month 2 · Build presence

  • Publish 1 locally-targeted blog post; set up Instagram and post 2–3×/week (short videos).
  • Launch a simple email capture + send your first monthly note.

Month 3 · Authority

  • Host one free talk/workshop via your university or a community group.
  • Quarterly thank-you + check-in with anyone who referred.
  • Review your "how did you hear about me?" data — double down on what's working.

06 Trainee & ethics caveats — read before you market

You're a PsyD student. Marketing as a trainee has rules that protect you and your clients:
  • Represent your status accurately — e.g., "PsyD trainee, supervised by Dr. ___." Don't imply full/independent licensure.
  • Confirm scope & supervision — check with your program/supervisor and licensing body (e.g., RCI in India, your state board in the US) whether and how you may see private clients while a student.
  • Testimonials: many ethics codes (e.g., APA 5.05) prohibit soliciting testimonials from current clients. The quote block on the homepage should use a past client with written consent, or be reframed (anonymised/aggregate). Google reviews are treated differently but still verify your code.
  • Confidentiality on social media — never reference identifiable client material; get explicit consent for anything.
  • Clear informed consent, fee transparency, and avoiding dual relationships when marketing to your own community.
Not legal advice — confirm specifics with your supervisor and licensing board.

Also note: a Google Business Profile typically needs a real service address. If you don't have a private clinic address yet, you can hide the address and set a service area, use a co-working/therapy-room address, or lean on directories until you do.
